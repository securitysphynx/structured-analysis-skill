# Protocol: Counterfactual Reasoning

> **Phase**: Challenge & Foresight | **Effort**: Hours | **Bias Mitigated**: Confirmation bias, Historical inevitability narratives
> **Output Artifact**: `working/counterfactual.md`
> **Library**: 00-prime §4 (Extended: Frontier), 04-agile-rigor-update

---

## Purpose

Look at "what might have been" to better understand "what is." By positing the smallest possible change to a critical past event and rigorously tracing its consequences, this technique exposes hidden causal dynamics and challenges deterministic thinking.

**Use in disinformation defense**: Deconstructs "historical inevitability" narratives by showing multiple plausible paths not taken, exposing the constructed nature of adversary stories.

---

## Execution

### 1. SETUP
- Read current analytical findings and historical context from prior technique outputs
- Identify past events or decisions that are critical drivers of the current situation
- Gather evidence on the causal significance of candidate pivot points from `working/evidence-registry.md`

### 2. PRIME
State: "I will now look at what might have been to understand what is — examining how a small change to a critical past event would have altered the present."

### 3. EXECUTE

1. **Identify the pivot point** — select a specific past event or decision that is a critical driver of the current state:
   - It must be a concrete, identifiable event (not a vague trend)
   - There must be evidence for its causal significance
   - It should be a point where alternative outcomes were genuinely possible
2. **Posit the SMALLEST possible change** — define a minimal, plausible alteration to the pivot event:
   - Not "everything was different" but ONE specific, bounded change
   - The change must be historically plausible (it could have happened)
   - Smaller changes produce more analytically useful counterfactuals
3. **Rigorously trace subsequent effects** — step through the consequences of the change from the pivot point forward to the present:
   - Each step must follow logically from the previous one
   - Support each step with evidence or precedent where possible
   - Distinguish between high-confidence and speculative links in the chain
4. **Compare counterfactual to actual** — map the differences between what happened and what might have happened across relevant dimensions
5. **Extract insights about current dynamics** — ask:
   - What does this reveal about the causal forces shaping the current situation?
   - Which factors were truly decisive vs. incidental?
   - What does this imply for forward-looking assessment?

### 4. ARTIFACT
Write `working/counterfactual.md` using the Counterfactual Reasoning template.

### 5. FINDINGS
Summarize: the key insights about causal dynamics, which factors proved decisive, and implications for the current assessment.

### 6. HANDOFF
Pass insights to the next technique or to final assessment compilation.

---

## Watch-Outs
- **Too-large counterfactuals**: "What if the entire political system was different?" is not useful. Insist on the smallest possible change — it forces precision.
- **Deterministic thinking**: If tracing effects leads to "things would have ended up the same anyway," challenge this. It may indicate confirmation bias.
- **Narrative seduction**: The counterfactual story must be analytically rigorous, not just a compelling alternative history. Every link needs justification.
- **Single-cause fallacy**: The pivot point is a lens, not the sole cause. Acknowledge the interaction of multiple causal factors.
